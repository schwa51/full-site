## What kind of game is it?

Electric Bastionland uses the basic system of Into the Odd but gives it a more developed campaign premise and setting.

Every character has experienced a Failed Career and is now part of a group that shares a substantial debt. The characters become treasure hunters because recovering something valuable may be their only realistic way to repay it.

Adventures may take place in Bastion, an enormous and contradictory city; the rural Deep Country; or the Underground, where machines, aliens, and physical reality become increasingly unreliable.

## Core system

Characters have Strength, Dexterity, and Charisma. Dangerous actions are resolved with a d20 Save, succeeding when the result is equal to or below the relevant attribute.

There is no general skill system. The Conductor decides whether an action succeeds automatically, requires a Save, or is impossible without a better approach.

Most situations are therefore resolved through description, investigation, equipment, and rulings rather than by searching the character sheet for a specific ability.

## Characters

A character’s highest and lowest attributes determine one of one hundred Failed Careers. Each career provides starting equipment, background details, and an unusual capability, possession, relationship, or complication.

The careers are highly varied: characters might be former revolutionaries, professional mourners, street judges, mockeries, psychics, experimental subjects, or practitioners of occupations that only make sense in Bastion.

This creates strong initial identities without adding many rules. Characters remain mechanically simple, but they generally begin with more personality and asymmetry than characters in Into the Odd.

The group’s shared debt and Debtholder provide an immediate common objective. A rival expedition also pursues the same treasure, creating pressure against excessive delay.

## Combat

Attacks automatically inflict damage. Weapons determine the damage die, armor reduces the result, and serious damage eventually reduces Strength and risks critical injury.

When several characters attack the same target, all damage dice are rolled but only the highest result is used. This prevents a large group from simply multiplying damage and encourages players to divide threats, create advantages, or attempt actions other than attacking.

Combat is quick and dangerous. There are concise rules for blast weapons, impaired attacks, morale, large groups, vehicles, structures, retreat, and combat maneuvers. The system supports tactical decisions, but not detailed character powers or grid-based combinations.

## Character progression

Electric Bastionland does not have ordinary experience points or character levels.

Characters can increase their Hit Protection through surviving injuries and receiving Scars. They may also acquire Oddities, equipment, property, followers, services, wealth, and permanent changes caused by the setting. Attributes can sometimes be altered through unusual procedures or expensive services.

Progression is therefore largely fictional and material. Characters become more established, stranger, wealthier, better connected, or physically changed rather than following a predetermined development path.

## Distinctive features

- One hundred Failed Careers create unusual starting characters.
- The group shares a debt and a Debtholder.
- A rival expedition creates urgency.
- Attacks automatically cause damage.
- Multiple attackers do not simply combine all their damage.
- Bastion is designed through principles and contradictions rather than fixed encyclopedic lore.
- Character change is driven by scars, possessions, relationships, and consequences.

## Practical considerations

Electric Bastionland is mechanically easy to learn and provides substantially more setting and character inspiration than Into the Odd. The Failed Careers give players distinctive identities while avoiding complicated character creation.

Its setting is intentionally surreal and does not always provide conventional explanations. The extensive GM guidance offers methods for creating locations and situations, but the Conductor is still expected to make frequent rulings and embrace unexpected results.

The shared debt supplies a clear reason for the group to remain together, although characters may still disagree about how to pursue or spend their earnings.

Players seeking strong level progression or extensive mechanical optimization will find little of it here. Players interested in strange characters, rapid play, and emergent consequences will find more support than in most minimalist systems.

Overall: Electric Bastionland is a fuller and more characterful version of the Into the Odd approach, trading conventional advancement for immediate identity, group pressure, and an unusually flexible setting.
