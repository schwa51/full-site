## What kind of game is it?

Savage Worlds is a general-purpose rules system rather than a single setting. The same core mechanics can be used for fantasy adventurers, modern investigators, science-fiction crews, superheroes, soldiers, western gunslingers, or mixed-genre campaigns.

A complete campaign normally combines the core rules with one of the following:

- A published Savage Setting, which supplies the world, character options, equipment, creatures, special rules, and adventures.
- A genre Companion, which expands the tools for areas such as fantasy, science fiction, horror, or supers.
- A custom setting created by the GM using the core rules and optional Setting Rules.

The chosen setting matters substantially. It determines available ancestries, technology, powers, equipment, campaign tone, and which optional rules are active.

## Core system

Attributes and skills are rated by die type, usually from d4 to d12. To attempt an action, roll the relevant Trait die.

Player characters and other major figures are Wild Cards. They roll both their Trait die and a separate d6 Wild Die, keeping the better result.

The usual target number is 4. Every four points above the target produces a raise, which often improves the result.

Dice can Ace: when a die rolls its maximum value, it is rolled again and added. This can repeat, allowing even a modest die to occasionally produce an exceptional result.

This creates a system that is easy to resolve but highly variable. Characters usually succeed reliably in their specialties, but surprising successes and failures remain common.

## Characters

Characters are built without classes. They are defined through:

- Five attributes
- Broad skills
- Ancestry
- Hindrances
- Edges
- Derived statistics
- Equipment
- Powers, when the setting permits them

Hindrances are flaws, obligations, personality traits, or complications. They help define the character and provide points during creation. Playing them during the campaign can also earn Bennies.

Edges are the main source of special abilities. They may improve combat, leadership, social influence, investigation, magic, survival, or another area. Two characters with similar attributes and skills can play very differently because of their Edges.

The system permits both simple and elaborate characters. A straightforward fighter may rely on a few broad skills and passive combat Edges, while a spellcaster, inventor, commander, or highly specialized combatant may have more interacting abilities.

## Bennies

Each player begins a session with several Bennies, represented by tokens.

Bennies can be spent to:

- Reroll Trait rolls
- Recover from being Shaken
- Soak incoming Wounds
- Redraw initiative
- Reroll damage
- Regain Power Points
- Occasionally influence a minor story detail

The GM awards additional Bennies for clever play, memorable moments, heroism, and engaging with Hindrances.

Bennies are central to the game rather than an optional bonus. They allow characters to take risks and recover from poor rolls, but they also create an element of resource management: spending one now may leave fewer available to prevent injury later.

## Combat

Combat uses a standard deck of playing cards for initiative. Each Wild Card or group of similar opponents receives a card every round. Higher cards act first, while Jokers provide bonuses and refresh the players’ Bennies.

Movement is measured in tabletop inches, though the game can also be played without miniatures. Characters may normally move and take one action, but they can attempt several actions in a round by accepting penalties.

Melee attacks target Parry. Ranged attacks usually target 4, modified by range, cover, lighting, and other circumstances.

Damage is compared with Toughness:

- Meeting Toughness leaves the target Shaken.
- Each raise above Toughness inflicts a Wound.
- Wild Cards can usually sustain three Wounds before becoming Incapacitated.
- Most ordinary enemies are Extras and are removed after a single Wound.

This distinction allows the game to place many lesser opponents on the table without tracking each one like a player character.

Combat includes rules for cover, called shots, grappling, suppressive fire, area attacks, mounted combat, vehicles, hazards, and many other situations. Players can also use Tests to distract or make opponents Vulnerable, and Support to improve an ally’s roll.

Combat is designed to move faster than many detailed tactical games, but larger encounters can still take time because of initiative cards, status effects, modifiers, rerolls, and exploding damage dice.

## Character progression

Savage Worlds does not use character levels in the usual sense. Characters receive an Advance, often after one or two sessions depending on the campaign.

An Advance can usually be spent to:

- Increase skills
- Gain a new Edge
- Improve an attribute at certain intervals
- Remove or reduce a Hindrance when appropriate
- Learn new powers or improve supernatural abilities

Characters also move through broad Ranks:

- Novice
- Seasoned
- Veteran
- Heroic
- Legendary

Some Edges and powers require a particular Rank. This creates visible progression without tying every improvement to a large level-up package.

Advancement is frequent and allows substantial customization. It can also reward careful planning, since many powerful Edges have prerequisite chains involving attributes, skills, Rank, or earlier Edges.

## Powers

Magic, psionics, superpowers, miracles, weird science, and similar abilities use a shared Powers system.

A power provides the mechanical effect, while its Trapping describes what it looks like in the setting. The same power might represent a fireball, psychic blast, swarm of nanites, divine judgment, or burst of superhuman energy.

Characters with an Arcane Background use an appropriate skill to activate powers and usually spend Power Points. Different settings may alter how powers work, which abilities are available, or whether they exist at all.

This approach keeps the core mechanics consistent across genres, though it can make supernatural abilities feel mechanically similar unless the setting adds distinctive Edges, limitations, or rules.

## Adventure subsystems

The core book includes several systems for scenes that do not need to be resolved through ordinary combat or a single roll:

- Dramatic Tasks for defusing bombs, rituals, rescues, hacking, and other time-sensitive challenges
- Quick Encounters for resolving secondary fights, journeys, heists, or other scenes rapidly
- Chases for vehicles, mounted pursuits, and foot races
- Social Conflicts for debates, trials, negotiations, and persuasion
- Mass Battles for warfare
- Networking for gathering information and favors
- Interludes for character stories during downtime or travel
- Allies for followers and groups controlled by the players

These allow the GM to vary how much mechanical detail a scene receives.

## How the setting changes the game

The core rules alone do not establish a campaign’s exact experience. A setting usually determines:

- Which Edges, Hindrances, ancestries, and powers are available
- Whether characters are ordinary, heroic, superhuman, or fragile
- How lethal combat is
- Whether healing is easy or difficult
- What equipment and technology exist
- Which Setting Rules are active
- Whether the campaign emphasizes tactical battles, investigation, horror, exploration, war, or pulp action

For example, a fantasy dungeon campaign and a science-fiction military campaign may share the same dice system but feel substantially different because of equipment, Edges, powers, enemies, and Setting Rules.

## Distinctive features

- Attributes and skills are represented by different die types.
- Wild Cards roll a Wild Die and survive several Wounds.
- Lesser enemies are Extras and require less bookkeeping.
- Dice can Ace repeatedly, producing dramatic results.
- Bennies allow rerolls, damage prevention, and other interventions.
- Initiative uses playing cards and changes every round.
- Edges provide class-like specialization without fixed classes.
- Powers use common mechanics with setting-specific descriptions.
- Numerous subsystems handle chases, mass battles, social conflicts, and dramatic tasks.
- Setting Rules allow the same engine to be tuned toward different tones.

## Practical considerations

Savage Worlds offers more character customization and tactical structure than a minimalist game while remaining lighter than many highly detailed systems. It can accommodate both straightforward characters and players who enjoy combining Edges, skills, equipment, and powers.

The rules are not equally simple in every part of play. Basic Trait rolls are easy, but combat may involve several temporary states, modifiers, Bennies, opposed rolls, raises, area templates, and Edge interactions. Players who know their own abilities generally do not need to master the entire system.

The game’s unpredictability is deliberate. Exploding dice can allow an ordinary opponent to cause severe damage or a poorly rated character to achieve an extraordinary result. Bennies reduce this volatility but do not remove it.

Because it is a toolkit, the quality and clarity of the selected setting matter. A published setting provides a more complete and coherent experience than simply announcing a genre and using the core book alone.

Overall: Savage Worlds is a flexible action-adventure system with frequent character improvement, substantial build options, statted opponents, and tactical combat. Its rules can support many genres, but the default style remains cinematic, fast-moving, and volatile rather than neutral.
