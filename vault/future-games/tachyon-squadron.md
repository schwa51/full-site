## What kind of game is it?

Tachyon Squadron is a game about starfighter pilots serving in a volunteer military force defending the independent Draconis system.

The campaign combines space combat with the lives of the pilots between missions. Characters fly patrols, escorts, interceptions, strikes, and rescue operations, then return to base to repair ships, recover from stress, deal with relationships, and get into trouble.

The setting draws heavily from military aviation stories and space opera. Starfighters behave more like aircraft from twentieth-century dogfights than realistic spacecraft, and the game is interested as much in squadron dynamics as in military hardware.

Every player character is a combat pilot. The system allows different personalities and specialties, but it is not designed for a mixed crew of pilots, engineers, diplomats, and ground troops.

## Core system

Tachyon Squadron uses Fate Core.

Actions are resolved by rolling four Fate dice, adding a skill rating, and comparing the result with either a fixed difficulty or an opponent’s roll.

Most actions fall into four categories:

- **Overcome:** Resolve an obstacle.
- **Create an Advantage:** Establish a useful circumstance or aspect.
- **Attack:** Inflict stress or consequences.
- **Defend:** Resist an attack or hostile action.

Characters and scenes are described with Aspects, short phrases that establish important facts. A player may spend a Fate Point to invoke a relevant aspect for a bonus, reroll, or other advantage. The GM may compel an aspect to introduce complications, awarding the player a Fate Point for accepting the trouble.

This means the system rewards players for actively using character traits, relationships, problems, and environmental details.

## Characters

Characters are built from Aspects, skills, stunts, stress, and Fate-point refresh.

Each pilot has five Aspects:

- A high concept describing who the pilot is.
- A decompression aspect describing both a healthy and unhealthy way of handling stress.
- Two relationship aspects connecting the pilot to other player characters.
- One freeform aspect.

The relationship aspects are created collaboratively. Every character therefore begins with mechanical ties to other members of the squadron, such as trust, rivalry, resentment, debt, loyalty, or romantic tension.

Skills are arranged in a pyramid. All pilots assign ratings to four dedicated spacefaring skills:

- **Gunnery:** Attacking with starfighter weapons.
- **Pilot:** Avoiding enemy fire and controlling the ship.
- **Tactics:** Maneuvering into favorable positions.
- **Technology:** Detection, electronic warfare, shields, and emergency repairs.

Players also select action and social skills, allowing one pilot to be a strong investigator, technician, leader, athlete, negotiator, or ground combatant.

Stunts provide defined exceptions or bonuses. Characters begin with personal stunts and a gear stunt, such as specialized equipment or a distinctive modification.

## Starfighter engagements

Starfighter combat uses a dedicated system called an Engagement. It is significantly more structured than standard Fate combat.

An Engagement proceeds through repeated phases:

- **Detection:** Opposing forces attempt to identify or conceal themselves.
- **Maneuver:** Pilots use Tactics to improve their position.
- **Action:** Pilots attack, repair, rescue allies, switch positions, or perform other actions.
- **End of round:** Ongoing effects and the tactical situation are updated.

Aircraft occupy relative tactical states rather than exact coordinates. A pilot may be:

- Undetected
- In a neutral position
- In an advantageous position
- On an enemy’s tail
- Being tailed by an enemy

A Maneuver Chart tracks these relationships. Tactics determines who can gain position, while Gunnery and Pilot determine whether attacks hit.

This separates several forms of pilot competence. A character may be an excellent shot but poor at maneuvering, or a skilled defensive pilot who struggles to place enemies in their sights.

## Ships, shields, and damage

Starfighters have shields that absorb damage before the pilot or ship suffers lasting consequences. Shields function similarly to a stress track and can be redirected or restored through Technology actions.

Once shields are depleted, attacks can cause consequences representing damaged engines, control failures, injured pilots, or failing life support.

Pilots may:

- Make standard attacks.
- Take snap shots from less favorable positions.
- Attack large ships or fixed targets.
- Repair damage during combat.
- Rescue ejected pilots.
- Shake an enemy off their tail.
- Abandon the engagement or eject.

Enemy fighters can be represented individually, as small Flights, or as larger Swarms. This lets the system handle several scales of combat without giving every hostile pilot a complete character sheet.

Combat emphasizes coordination. Pilots can create openings, protect squadron mates, break up enemy formations, or concentrate attacks on a priority target.

## Stress and consequences

Characters have a stress track that functions more like hit points than standard Fate stress. Stress does not automatically clear after every scene.

Pilots accumulate stress through combat, injuries, fear, fatigue, personal conflict, and operational pressure. More serious harm becomes a Consequence, represented by an Aspect such as Concussed, Damaged Flight Controls, or Confidence Shaken.

Consequences can be invoked by opponents until they are treated and allowed to recover.

Stress must be reduced deliberately during off-duty scenes. This makes the period between missions mechanically important rather than simple narration.

## Off-duty play and decompression

Between missions, each pilot chooses activities such as:

- Healthy decompression
- Unhealthy “raising hell”
- Sleeping and recovering
- Repairing or modifying a ship
- Training
- Shopping
- Investigating local problems
- Spending time with another pilot

Healthy decompression may be safer but less efficient. Unhealthy decompression can clear stress quickly while creating legal, romantic, financial, or interpersonal trouble.

The decompression aspect determines what healthy and unhealthy coping look like for each character. Because two of every pilot’s other Aspects are relationships, downtime naturally produces scenes involving squadron mates rather than isolated personal errands.

The game can therefore shift between a structured combat procedure and relatively freeform character drama.

## Character progression

Tachyon Squadron uses Fate’s milestone advancement rather than experience points or levels.

At smaller milestones, players may adjust parts of the character to reflect recent events, such as:

- Rewriting an Aspect
- Changing a stunt
- Moving skill ratings
- Recovering from certain consequences

More significant milestones can allow a skill increase, a new stunt, or an increase in Fate-point refresh. Major campaign achievements can produce broader changes to the character and the setting.

Progression is flexible and tied to the story, but less numerically visible than gaining levels. Characters usually become broader, better defined, or more specialized rather than gaining large increases in durability or damage.

## Campaign structure

A typical session or campaign cycle follows a recognizable routine:

- Mission briefing
- Launch and travel
- Starfighter engagement or operational challenge
- Return to base
- Repairs, recovery, and decompression
- Personal scenes and complications
- Preparation for the next mission

Missions can include fighter sweeps, interceptions, escort duty, strike missions, patrols, reconnaissance, and search-and-rescue operations.

Campaigns may be episodic or organized around a larger military objective. The setting includes rival powers, pirates, local politics, competing squadrons, and the strain of defending an underfunded independent system.

## Distinctive features

- Every player character is a fighter pilot.
- Starfighter combat uses a dedicated phased Engagement system.
- Gunnery, Pilot, Tactics, and Technology each govern different parts of aerial combat.
- Relative position and maneuvering matter before attacks can be made effectively.
- Characters begin with two mechanical relationships to other pilots.
- Stress persists between scenes and must be managed off duty.
- Healthy and unhealthy coping methods are built into character creation.
- Downtime can create complications as important as combat outcomes.
- Enemy groups can be modeled as individual fighters, Flights, or Swarms.

## Practical considerations

Tachyon Squadron provides more mechanical structure for vehicle combat than most Fate games. Players who enjoy distinct tactical roles, maneuvering, and teamwork may find Engagements satisfying.

However, all characters share the same basic profession. Differentiation comes through skill emphasis, stunts, personality, and relationships rather than through radically different classes or careers. Some players may feel that everyone is performing variations of the same role.

The game also expects players to engage actively with Fate’s Aspects, invokes, compels, and collaborative character relationships. Players who prefer abilities to have fixed, literal effects may find parts of the system abstract or dependent on negotiation.

Progression is meaningful but largely horizontal. A pilot may gain new stunts, improve skills, and change relationships, but the game does not provide a strong sequence of levels, equipment tiers, or steadily escalating character powers.

Finally, Engagements are specialized. They provide depth for starfighter combat, but campaigns that spend little time in the cockpit would leave much of the game’s distinctive system unused.

Overall: Tachyon Squadron is a focused military space-opera game built around structured dogfights and the personal lives of the pilots who survive them. It is strongest when the group wants repeated starfighter missions, close squadron relationships, and character drama shaped by the pressure of war.
