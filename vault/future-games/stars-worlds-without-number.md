## What kind of system is it?

Stars Without Number and Worlds Without Number share the same basic system.

Stars Without Number supports science-fiction campaigns involving planetary exploration, mercenary work, trade, political conflict, lost technology, psychic powers, and starships.

Worlds Without Number adapts the system for fantasy, with wilderness exploration, ancient ruins, faction conflict, magic, and the remnants of advanced civilizations. Its default Latter Earth setting is science fantasy, but most of the rules can support more conventional fantasy.

Both games are designed primarily for sandbox campaigns in which the players choose objectives and the world responds to their decisions. They can also support a more directed campaign.

## Core system

Different situations use different dice:

- Most skill checks use 2d6 plus a skill and attribute modifier against a difficulty.
- Combat attacks use d20 plus bonuses against Armor Class.
- Saving throws use a d20 and must meet or exceed the character’s save score.

The skill system produces a predictable range of results, while combat retains recognizable D&D-style mechanics.

The games include more rules and character options than Dragonbane or Into the Odd, but the core procedures remain relatively conventional.

## Characters

Characters choose a background, skills, a class, and Foci, which are special talents or areas of expertise.

The main classes are:

- **Warrior:** strongest in direct combat.
- **Expert:** particularly effective with skills and problem-solving.
- **Psychic or Mage:** uses supernatural powers.
- **Adventurer:** combines partial versions of two classes.

Foci allow further specialization in combat, social influence, exploration, technical skills, leadership, stealth, or other areas. A character can be straightforward or involve substantial planning, depending on the class and options selected.

The broad skill list allows characters to develop outside their main specialty, although class abilities still give each character a recognizable strength.

## Combat

Combat uses initiative, attack rolls, Armor Class, damage, movement, and distinct action types. Characters normally receive a Main Action and a Move Action, with additional quick or reactive actions available in some situations.

Weapons and enemies have conventional statistics. Melee weapons may inflict Shock damage even when an attack misses, making close combat more decisive and reducing repeated rounds in which nothing happens.

Combat is dangerous, particularly at low levels. Firearms in Stars Without Number and mobs or heavy melee weapons in Worlds Without Number can incapacitate inexperienced characters quickly. Scouting, cover, preparation, negotiation, and retreat remain important even as characters become more capable.

## Character progression

Characters gain experience points and advance through ten levels. Advancement increases hit points, combat ability, saving throws, skill points, and access to additional Foci or class features.

This creates clear and visible improvement. Characters become more reliable and gain new options, though the power curve is less extreme than in many modern fantasy games.

Both books include optional rules for more heroic campaigns. Stars Without Number can support space-opera heroes, artificial intelligences, mechs, transhuman characters, and space magic. Worlds Without Number includes heroic characters and eventual advancement into superhuman Legates.

## Stars Without Number

The science-fiction version adds:

- Psychic disciplines and techniques.
- Starship construction, modification, travel, and combat.
- Technology levels, cyberware, robots, vehicles, and advanced equipment.
- Planet and sector creation.
- Interstellar factions whose actions alter the campaign.
- Optional rules for trade, mechs, artificial intelligence, transhumanism, and space magic.

Starship combat assigns characters to departments such as command, engineering, gunnery, communications, and bridge operations. Players generate and spend Command Points to power ship actions. This gives several players responsibilities during a battle, but it is a separate subsystem that requires more learning than ordinary combat.

## Worlds Without Number

The fantasy version adds:

- Several magical traditions, each combining spells with reusable Arts.
- Wilderness travel, exploration, encumbrance, and survival procedures.
- Crafting, magical items, creatures, and dungeon-building tools.
- Renown and Major Projects for characters attempting large-scale changes.
- Faction rules for kingdoms, cults, armies, and other organizations.
- Compatibility with many older fantasy adventures and monsters.

The default setting includes ancient technology, alien influence, magical ruins, and altered landscapes, but the system can be used for low fantasy, sword-and-sorcery, traditional fantasy, or higher-powered heroic games.

## Practical considerations

The shared system provides more character customization and more visible advancement than the other games considered here. It can support both straightforward characters and characters with more complicated powers or specializations.

The books are large because they contain extensive GM tools and optional systems. Players do not need to learn everything, but the group must decide which subsystems—such as starship combat, faction turns, detailed travel, crafting, trade, or Major Projects—will be central.

The default rules assume a relatively dangerous world that is not balanced around the characters’ level. A clear campaign objective and characters willing to work together are especially useful in an open sandbox.

Overall: The Without Number system combines recognizable level-based advancement and statted opposition with flexible characters and unusually extensive support for building a player-directed campaign. Stars emphasizes technology, ships, and interstellar exploration; Worlds emphasizes fantasy, magic, wilderness, and ancient ruins.
