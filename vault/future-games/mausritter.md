## What kind of game is it?

Mausritter is a fantasy adventure game about small mice exploring a world built at a much larger scale.

Characters leave the safety of mouse settlements to explore ruins, wilderness, human structures, burrows, and forgotten places. Threats may include rival mice, rats, insects, snakes, cats, weather, hunger, and human objects that become enormous pieces of terrain.

The tone can be warm and whimsical, but the rules treat danger seriously. Clever planning and avoidance are often more reliable than direct confrontation.

## Core system

Characters have three attributes:

- Strength
- Dexterity
- Will

When a character attempts something dangerous, the player makes a Save by rolling a d20 equal to or below the relevant attribute.

Safe or reasonable actions usually succeed without a roll. Saves are used to avoid clearly stated consequences rather than to determine whether every ordinary action works.

There is no skill list. A character’s capabilities are based on attributes, background, inventory, equipment, spells, allies, and the way the player approaches a problem.

## Characters

Character creation is fast and partly random. Players roll attributes, Hit Protection, starting money, background, equipment, appearance, and small personal details.

Backgrounds include occupations such as blacksmith, hedge witch, beetle herder, trap thief, cartographer, librarian, sewer guide, or kitchen forager. These provide starting possessions rather than permanent classes.

Characters therefore begin with recognizable identities but are not restricted to fixed roles. What a mouse can do later depends heavily on the items, spells, allies, and opportunities acquired during play.

## Inventory and conditions

Mausritter uses a physical slot-based inventory system.

Items occupy spaces for the paws, body, and pack. Large items may take two slots. Retrieving something from the pack during combat requires an action.

Conditions such as Exhausted, Hungry, Frightened, or Injured are represented by cards placed directly into inventory slots. They reduce the character’s carrying capacity until cleared through rest, food, safety, or another specific remedy.

Most expendable items have three usage dots. After a fight or significant use, a die roll may mark usage. Once all dots are marked, the item is depleted or broken.

This makes equipment, fatigue, and injury visible parts of character management rather than separate abstract tracks.

## Combat

Attacks automatically hit. The attacker rolls the weapon’s damage die and subtracts armor.

Damage first reduces Hit Protection. Once Hit Protection is depleted, further damage reduces Strength. After taking Strength damage, the target makes a Strength Save; failure causes critical damage and incapacitation.

Characters may move and take one action per round. Actions can include attacking, fleeing, negotiating, disarming, tripping, or interacting with the environment.

Attacks can become:

- Impaired, reducing damage to d4.
- Enhanced, increasing damage to d12.

Combat is fast and dangerous. Because attacks always hit, entering a fight guarantees attrition. Tactical play focuses on surprise, positioning, equipment, numbers, environmental advantages, and deciding whether combat is worth the risk.

Creatures have concise but concrete statistics, including Hit Protection, attributes, armor, damage, and distinctive behavior.

## Character progression

Mausritter uses experience points and levels.

Characters earn one XP for every pip of treasure or useful goods brought safely back to a mouse settlement, divided among the party. They can also earn XP by spending wealth on improvements that benefit the wider community.

At each new level, the character:

- Rolls for possible attribute increases.
- Rolls additional Hit Dice to improve maximum Hit Protection.
- Gains increasing Grit, allowing some Conditions to be stored without occupying normal inventory slots.

Advancement is recognizable and visible, although new levels mainly improve survivability rather than provide extensive new powers.

A large portion of development also comes from spells, magic weapons, equipment, hirelings, warbands, constructions, wealth, and relationships with settlements and factions.

## Magic

Spells are physical tablets carried in inventory.

Casting a spell requires rolling dice based on its current power. The spell may lose usage depending on the result, and stronger casting can increase both the effect and the risk of depletion.

Spell tablets can be recharged by performing a specific action unique to each spell. This turns recovery into a small objective or adventure prompt rather than a standard rest mechanic.

Magic is useful and strange, but limited by inventory space and usage.

## Exploration and campaigns

The game includes procedures for:

- Dungeon and adventure-site exploration.
- Wilderness travel by hex.
- Time tracking through rounds, turns, and watches.
- Weather, foraging, rest, and random encounters.
- Settlements, factions, rumors, and hexcrawl creation.
- Recruiting hirelings, creating warbands, and building constructions.

Travel is handled collectively rather than assigning one permanent navigator role. The group decides where to move and how to spend each watch, while individual Saves arise from hazards, weather, or specific choices.

The campaign structure naturally links expeditions to settlement life: recover treasure, return to safety, improve the mice or community, and venture out again.

## Distinctive features

- Conditions physically occupy inventory space.
- Item cards and usage dots make equipment management tangible.
- Attacks automatically hit.
- Treasure returned to safety directly produces XP.
- Spending money on community improvements also produces XP.
- Spells are carried as physical tablets with unique recharge requirements.
- The mouse-scale setting turns familiar objects into strange terrain and resources.
- Rules support hirelings, warbands, settlements, factions, and constructions.

## Practical considerations

Mausritter is easy to learn while offering more explicit advancement and campaign structure than many minimalist games. Its inventory system creates meaningful choices without requiring a long list of character abilities.

Characters begin mechanically simple. Differentiation comes primarily from backgrounds, equipment, magic, scars, followers, and discoveries rather than character builds.

The game is lethal, especially at low levels. Its charming presentation should not be mistaken for a low-risk game. Players are expected to gather information, use the environment, negotiate, and avoid unfavorable fights.

The default campaign style is exploratory and player-directed. It can support a more focused story, but its rules and GM tools are built around maps, factions, dangerous locations, treasure, and returning to settlements.

Overall: Mausritter combines the speed and danger of Into the Odd with clearer level advancement, structured exploration, a highly concrete inventory system, and a setting that makes even familiar environments feel unusual.
